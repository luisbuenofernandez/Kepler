What motivates you?
To answer the interview question "What motivates you?", it is important to focus on the genuine reasons that drive you to work and seek the specific position you are applying for.

Here is an example of a response in the first person:

I am motivated by the opportunity to [describe a challenge or problem you are passionate about]. I believe my skills and experience in [mention your relevant skills and experience] allow me to contribute significantly to [describe how you can add value to the company].

Specifically, I am motivated by [describe a specific aspect of the position or company that attracts you]. For example, if you are motivated by the company's culture of innovation, you might say: "I am motivated by the culture of innovation that is fostered in this company, where new ideas are valued and given the opportunity to become a reality."

It is important to be concrete and specific in your response. Do not simply say that you are motivated by "money" or "success." Explain what you are passionate about and how your motivations align with the company's goals.

In addition to the above, you can also mention some past achievements or experiences that demonstrate your motivation. For example, you could talk about a project you worked hard on that gave you great satisfaction or a time when you overcame a challenge.

Here are some additional tips for answering the question "What motivates you?":
  • Be honest and authentic. Do not invent an answer you think the interviewer wants to hear.
  • Be positive and enthusiastic. Show the interviewer that you are motivated and excited about the opportunity to work at the company.
  • Connect your answer to the position. Explain how your motivations will help you succeed in the role.
  • Prepare specific examples. Share stories or anecdotes that support your motivations.
  • Practice your response. Rehearse your answer with a friend or family member to ensure it sounds natural and convincing.

Here are some additional tips for answering the question "Where do you see yourself in 5 years?":

The number of questions you prepare for a job interview is not a definitive indicator of your preparedness. What really matters is that you understand the types of questions that are commonly asked, that you can tailor your answers to the specific situation, and that you demonstrate confidence and enthusiasm for the opportunity.

That said, preparing between 20 and 30 generic questions can be a good starting point. This will allow you to cover a wide range of topics and be ready to answer most of the interviewer's questions.

Here are some tips for preparing your questions:

Research the company and the position. The more you know about the company and the position, the better you will be able to tailor your questions. Look for information on the company's website, in news articles, and on social media.
Read about common interview questions. There are many resources available online and in libraries that can help you prepare for the most common questions.
Practice your answers. Once you have some questions, practice your answers out loud. This will help you feel more confident and sound more natural during the interview.
Tailor your questions to the specific situation. Not all questions will be appropriate for every interview. Make sure to tailor your questions to the company, the position, and your own experience.
Prepare some questions about the company and the position. This will show the interviewer that you are interested in the opportunity and that you have done your research.
